University of the Pacific  - D1, Class of 2004     

1.  What suggestions do you have to pre-dental students getting ready for dental school?

In preparing for dental school I think it is important to take as many of the subjects that are on the National Boards (Part I) as possible.  It will help you with the exam (probably taken your 2nd year of dental school) plus it will help with the classes you have to take during your 1st year.  It will help because of the load you have the 1st year. Individually the classes are not incredibly difficult, but collectively it is hard to manage your time to do well in each course.  The classes are: histology, biochemistry, gross anatomy (any anatomy classes will help), physiology, and microbiology. In addition, it would be VERY useful to have some sort of lab experience.  It is important to volunteer in dental clinics and do as much assisting as possible, but it would also be helpful to work in a dental laboratory.  At UOP, you do as much lab work (cavity preps, crown preps, crown model work and casting, etc.) as didactic work your 1st year, and the people that have lab experience definitely have an edge. 

2.  Do you feel you have made the right decision on the school you are attending? 

UOP is the PERFECT environment to go through such a rigorous curriculum.  The overall attitude of the building every single day is extremely positive.  We are all part of a family, and everyone looks out for everyone else.  I do not feel competition among the classes, which is rare in a program such as dental school.  Pretty much any dental school you go to in the country, you will be a "qualified" dentist, but it is my strong belief that you should go to the school you feel the most comfortable regardless of the cost, location, reputation, etc.  Your success as a student and inevitably as a professional will be best at a place you enjoy going to every day!!!  That will be the best advice anyone can ever give you about picking a school.  I have never thought twice about my decision to come to UOP!  For the way I learn and for me to be the best student I can be, I feel this school is the best school in the country! 

3.  What are some strengths and weaknesses of the school? 

It is the opinion of some (although not held by me) that one of the weaknesses of UOP is the three-year curriculum. They think that you get less of an education because of the one-year difference. However, instead of completing 12 quarters in four calendar years, we complete 12 quarters in three calendar years by having a summer quarter that is full time. There are numerous strengths of UOP. Each year, the Admissions Committee surveys the incoming class to learn why they chose UOP. The "Top Ten" reasons have remained constant over the last several years. They are listed on the UOP website, but here they are: 

 10. Three-year program: The ability to complete four academic years of instruction in just three calendar years appeals to both recent graduates and second career students. Many note that UOP's program enables them to enter general practice or begin specialty education one year earlier than any other dental program. 

9. Strong clinical and practice management emphasis: Nearly all first-year students indicate that the strong clinical orientation of the school is a major factor in their decision to attend UOP. 

8. Reputation of UOP for producing excellent clinicians: Students report that recommendations made by dentists, students, and pre-dental advisors significantly influenced their decision to attend UOP. 

7. Humanistic orientation: Students note that faculty treat them as peers starting on the first day of school. UOP places much emphasis on the development of self-confidence and self-esteem. 

6. Superb facility: Students like the physical facility, which they describe as state-of-the-art, attractive, and "clean." UOP's new simulation lab sets the worldwide standard for preclinical education. Students rank UOP's facility and environment above other dental schools. 

5. Location: San Francisco is one of the most unique and beautiful cities in the world. UOP's location in Pacific Heights is considered to be a major advantage, particularly as a place to live while in dental school. 

4. Quality of administration, faculty, and staff: Applicants are impressed by faculty availability and the quality of instruction. Also, many reported being impressed by their contact with Dean Dugoni and administrators as well as staff who are praised consistently for being friendly, helpful, and supportive. 

3. Curriculum: Students like the integration of basic and clinical sciences that defines the UOP curriculum. They also prefer the early clinical experience which starts on the first day of school. 

2. Emphasis on competencies: Students prefer UOP's competency-based instruction and evaluation system. Competency-based evaluation is designed to measure the full range of knowledge, skills, and professional values necessary to begin the independent practice of dentistry. 

1. "UOP family": Students become part of the UOP family on the first day of school. They like the interaction with UOP alumni and being treated as a peer by all. Many students report that dentists have told them that alumni stay involved with the school throughout their professional careers. The collaborative, non-competitive relationships of current dental students also appeal to applicants. 

4.  How is the student/teacher ratio?  What is your impression of the faculty? 

The student faculty ratio is 11:1 in lab classes. (We are separated into rows in the simulation laboratory, and each row has a row instructor.) In other didactic classes, the ratio varies, but there is always plenty of help offered whenever it is needed. The faculty treat you as a colleague at UOP. That is part of the "humanistic" environment. The criticism is ALWAYS constructive and never demeaning. They are so willing to help you at all times! There are row instructors that stay after school or come in on a weekend to help students. They also will give up their lunches to help you understand a concept you are missing! They are truly there to help you to learn! 

5.  Do you get enough clinical experience? 

The emphasis at UOP is clinical dentistry. This is demonstrated by how quickly you are practicing clinical dentistry. In my first week at this school, we were cutting cavity preparations with handpieces in my operative dentistry class. We were also practicing an anterior PFM crown in my restorative dentistry class. Most schools do not even see a high-speed handpiece until their second year. (Which again proves that we get just as much experience as four year programs…1 year of pre-clinical dentistry and 2 years of clinical dentistry practice.) Our clinic experience also starts early (the first quarter of your 2nd year). By the end of your 2nd year, you have already done crowns, bridges, endodontic therapy, and periodontic surgeries. 

6.  How competitive is the class?  Are classes with medical students? 

As I said before, there is very little competition within any class at UOP. Everyone is there to help everyone else. Test files are passed down from class to class, lecture notes are transcribed and posted for the entire class after difficult lectures, and tutors are available for people struggling in any given class. There are no medical students in any of our classes. 

7.  How's city life and the weather? 

Living in San Francisco is wonderful! It actually reminds me a lot of  Seattle, just much bigger!! Overall, the weather is comfortable;  although, it is not like typical California weather. It is much cooler than many of the other neighboring cities. But it rarely rains and is often in the 60’s (even in January!). The really cool part about living in California is that you can drive in any direction and see different things. The beaches, wine country, Monterey and Carmel,  Great America, Lake Tahoe, even Los Angeles are all within driving  range.
