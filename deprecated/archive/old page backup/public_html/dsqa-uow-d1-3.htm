University of Washington - D1, Class of 2005 

1.  What suggestions do you have to pre-dental students getting ready for dental school?      

Volunteer at a clinic.  The UW is a great place to do this, because there's a lot of clinics, and versus private practices, clinics generally allow the volunteers to have more responsibilities.  Also, dentists in the UW clinics may be involved with teaching at the UW, and may care more about the advancement of your education.  Also, it will provide you with a picture of what being a dentist is about.  (It's nice to know what an amalgam, composite, endo, inlay, onlay, crown, etc are.)      2) Try to get a job in a dental laboratory.  Your first two years of Dental School will focus on lab work.  If you can get good experience working at a lab, you will be miles ahead of most dental students. This was the most frustrating part of my first quarter, and is something I wish I had done.

2.  Do you feel you have made the right decision on the school you are attending? 

For me, yes.  I like Seattle, and the UW.  It should be known that the faculty here do not pat you on the back, or sugar-coat things. Generally, they're pretty nice, but they can also be pretty harsh.  I've heard other schools are much kinder, but I like where I am, and my classmates.  At UW, you're expected to learn a lot on your own; it's a different world from undergrad.

 3.  What are some strengths and weaknesses of the school?     

This is tough to talk about, because I have nothing to compare it to.  The school has a good reputation.  The lab is supposed to be one of the best in the country.  Weaknesses are also tough to describe, because I don't know what is important to me, and what is not.  Right now, we have basic science classes which seem to involve details that aren't important to dentists (i.e.: what's palpable in a rectal exam?  the development of the external genitalia?  Bronchopulmonary segments?)  I kind of wish the classes would involve what we need to know with more focus on the things we will use. 

4.  How is the student/teacher ratio?  What is your impression of the  faculty? 

I appreciate the faculty, because they almost volunteer their time to our learning.  They could be having more fun (like working and earning more money, golfing, spending more time with family), but they are there for us.  Although they don't make dental school easy, without them we'd be lost.  The ratio is okay, but you have to understand that there's a nation-wide shortage of faculty (and dentists).  I think there is enough faculty available for help, but it's isn't always easy to find someone.  I wouldn't say it's a major problem, though.

5.  Do you get enough clinical experience?    

First quarter we got no clinical experience.  That's why volunteering can be valuable.  This quarter we will be spending some time in the emergency clinic, and looking at our classmates mouths.  Right now, we don't have the skills to be in clinic, but we do have the opportunity to assist 3rd and 4th years.  I haven't done that, because I think my volunteer experience would be close to the same thing.  The faculty and dean understand that we'd like to be in clinic, but we don't have the skill.  Clinic time will come, and the curriculum is trying to get us in their more than past years. 

6.  How competitive is the class?  Are classes with medical students?

There's a handful of competitive people, but it's not like the med school where every student is trying to beat the others.  There are classes with the med students, but the UW med school is pass/fail/honors, so there isn't a whole lot of pressure.

7.  How's city life and the weather?

I think most people know whether or not they like cities.  So it's a personal choice.  I've gotten use to the weather, so the rain doesn't bother me much.  Also, last quarter I didn't have to worry about missing out on a nice sunny day, because I went to class before the sun had fully risen, spent all day inside, and left the health sciences building after dark. 

8.  Additional comments

Dental school is a lot different from undergrad at UW; it's a different world.  I took some hard quarters at UW, but they really don't compare.  For example, in Spring 2000 I took Biology, O Chem, and Physics.  It was hard, but I did well.  Fall 2000 I took Bio-chem, Microbiology 402 & 410, Social Psych, and Classics 205, and that wasn't nearly as bad as dental school (as far as the work to success ratio).  However, your experience at dental school can be improved if you work at a lab and get good experience, and are able to manage your time well.  Thanks for reading!
